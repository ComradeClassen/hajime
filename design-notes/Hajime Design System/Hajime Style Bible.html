01i.

Why this comes before the deck

The old pitch deck inherited its visual language by default — generic dark UI, neon stat panels, sci‑fi grid. It read like every other indie sim. Twenty minutes here saves a full deck rewrite later. Lock the bible. The deck inherits.

RULE · 01
Judo, not e-sports.

Hajime is a sport with deep roots, formal etiquette, and an understated culture. The writing respects that — and so does the visual system. No hype voice. No announcer. No neon. No drop shadows pretending to be depth.

RULE · 02
The dojo is the default surface.

Washi paper, sumi ink, tatami green, the single vermilion stamp. The interface reads as a sensei's notebook first, a sports broadcast second, a video game last. Even the deck.

RULE · 03
Physics resolves; prose marks.

The simulation never announces itself. The visual system never announces itself either. Hairlines, not borders. Italic asides, not callouts. One stamp of color, not three.

RULE · 04
Either choice is okay — but commit.

Two typeface candidates. One palette. One hierarchy. Pick now, cut the others, document the reasoning. The discipline is what makes the system feel like one thing across two years of building.

02ii.

Mission for the deck

One paragraph. Everything the deck must communicate. Everything else is noise.

MISSION · ONE PARAGRAPH

HAJIME is a coaching simulator about running a judo dojo across decades — a Football Manager for a sport that values restraint, where the central mechanic is the scarcity of your attention. The deck must communicate three things: that the dojo loop is the game (not the matches that happen inside it); that the simulation is real all the way down (combinatorial physics, a grip graph, students with hidden inner lives); and that this is a 2–3 year solo build by a designer who is choosing the right shape of project over a faster one. The reader should leave understanding that Hajime is not "a judo game" — it is the systemic biography of an institution, told through one sensei's working life.

Tone: the deck reads like the master document feels. Quiet, specific, confident. No hype voice. Every claim is grounded in a system that exists or will. The reader should feel like a knowledgeable friend has just sat down with them and walked them carefully through what they have been building.

03iii.

Palette & named uses

Six colors. Each with a job. The vermilion is the only loud one — and it stays rare on purpose, the way a referee's flag stays rare. Whites and blacks are warm, paper-toned, never sterile.

Washi 和紙
#F3EDE0 · oklch(.93 .017 84)
Primary surface. The page. The mat. Default everything that is not text.
Washi-2 和紙弐
#E8E0CD
Recessed surface. Cards, panels, the hierarchy of "this is contained."
Sumi 墨
#15140F · oklch(.18 .01 80)
All primary text. Hairline rules at full strength. The voice of the document.
Tatami 畳
#6F7A4A
Institutional. The dojo loop, calendar, schedule. Where work gets done.
Shu 朱
#B8331F · oklch(.51 .163 33)
Vermilion. Referee, score, the stamp. Use sparingly. Less than 5% of any view.
Shinchu 真鍮
#B08A3A
Aged brass. Promotions, lineage, awards. Even rarer than vermilion.
Rule 罫
#C4B896
Hairline rules, dividers, table grid. Quiet structure.
Tatami-deep 深畳
#4D5634
Headers on tatami panels. Charts. Long shadows.
No pure black. No pure white. by intent
Sumi #15140F is warm-tinted near-black; Washi #F3EDE0 is the color of unbleached paper. The result is a document that reads as made by a human in a real room, not generated by software in the dark.
Tatami green is the institutional voice. not the loud one
The dojo's identity color is green, because tatami is green, because the calmest part of the room is the most important part. Football Manager uses purple for the same reason — calendar, work, structure.
Vermilion is the score. and only that
Shu #B8331F appears: match score state, referee actions, the antagonist's escalation, error states, the cover-stamp. Never as a section background. Never as a button fill on more than one button per view.
Brass is the lineage. rarer still
Shinchu appears: belt promotions, the founding lineage seal, championship medals, the succession event. If the deck has no slide about lineage, brass does not appear in the deck.
04iv.

Typeface candidates

Three serif families auditioned. Each pairs with one workhorse body and one mono. Recommendation marked. The deck commits to one before slide one is laid out.

Candidate A — display
Fraunces
+ Source Serif 4 (body) · JetBrains Mono (data)
The twins
arrive.
Within the first month of a new dojo, two boys walk in together. The recognition lands. The lineage is felt history now, not a stat.
Why. Variable optical sizes mean one family handles the 96px masthead and the 18px section kicker without a second face. Soft humanist contrast — no Didone glamour, no slab toughness. Reads as literary, not corporate. Strong italic. Pairs with Source Serif's quieter body voice.
Candidate B — display
Shippori Mincho
+ Source Serif 4 (body) · JetBrains Mono (data)
The twins
arrive.
Within the first month of a new dojo, two boys walk in together. The recognition lands. The lineage is felt history now, not a stat.
Why consider. A Japanese mincho with a working Latin set. Signals the cultural register honestly — the game is about a Japanese sport. Why not lead with it. Mincho's Latin glyphs are pleasant but not as expressive in headlines; risks reading as a "Japan‑themed product" rather than a serious work. Better used as an accent (chapter numerals, the 始め stamp, JP captions).
Candidate C — display
Fraunces SOFT
+ Inter (body) · JetBrains Mono (data)
The twins
arrive.
Within the first month of a new dojo, two boys walk in together. The recognition lands. The lineage is felt history now, not a stat.
Why consider. Same family as A, but rounder optical axis + Inter for body. More approachable, more game, less essay. Why not lead with it. Inter is on every SaaS landing page in 2026. The deck should not look like one.
Display Fraunces · 300–600 · opsz 144
Slide titles, section heads, the masthead. Italic for specific words inside titles — twins, hajime, sensei. Never used below 22px.
Body Source Serif 4 · 400–600 · opsz 14
Paragraph text, captions, slide bodies. Generous leading (1.5–1.55). Pretty text-wrap on. Max 64ch line length.
Data & UI JetBrains Mono · 400–500
Match logs, stat panels, calendar grid, build-status tags, eyebrow labels. Letter-spacing 0.14–0.22em uppercase for all-caps labels.
Accent Shippori Mincho · 400–600
Reserved for actual Japanese characters: 始め on the cover, 道場 in section dividers, calligraphy plates over photo. Never set Latin in Mincho in this system.
Forbidden do not use
Inter for headlines, Roboto anywhere, Arial, Helvetica Neue, system-ui, Montserrat, Poppins. They are the visual language of the old deck and every other indie deck. We are not them.

06vi.

Three voices, one system

From the master doc's tone rules. The bible carries them so the deck does not have to invent a voice mid-slide.

VOICE · MATCH LOG
Knowledgeable sportswriter
0:18 Tanaka attempts seoi-nage —
→ Sato sprawls. Hips back. Throw stuffed.
→ Slight scramble. Ne-waza window: 11%.
→ Both stand. Grips reset.
VOICE · COACH WINDOW
Intimate, slightly worried
"He went for his strongest throw too early. Sato read it. The grip fight is going against him on the right side."
VOICE · DOJO PROSE
Sunday evening, the notepad
Marco showed up early again. He has been quiet about the tournament — three conversations in and I still don't know if he wants to go. His grip is improving but only against the lighter partners.
07vii.

The two calls — 始め and 待て

Hajime and Matte are not labels in this system. They are typographic events. The whole interface — game, deck, marketing — bends around them. The bible commits to how they feel before any surface uses them.

CALL · 01 · THE BEGINNING
始め Hajime.
The referee's call that starts the match — and the call that names the game. When Hajime fires on a surface, everything else recedes. The world the player was watching becomes the world the player is in.
SCALE180–280px display. Larger than any L1 title in the system. Always the largest object on its surface.
FACEShippori Mincho 700 for 始め · Fraunces italic 300 for the romaji. Set together, never separately.
COLORSumi for the kanji, Shu for the romaji. The only place the two colors touch each other directly.
MOTIONIn-game: snaps in, holds 800ms, decays to a corner timestamp. In static media: full-bleed, alone, no caption above it.
DEMANDSAttention. The player's eye is taken. The next thing they see is consequential.
FREQUENCYOnce per round, once per session intro, once on the deck cover, once on the press kit. Always feels like the first thing.
CALL · 02 · THE PAUSE
待て Matte.
The referee's call that suspends the match. Equal weight to Hajime, opposite job. When Matte fires, the player exhales. The action stops. The chair becomes a place to think.
SCALESame as Hajime. Never smaller. Same display rectangle. The pair is symmetric.
FACEShippori Mincho 500 (lighter weight than Hajime) · Fraunces italic 300 in tatami-deep.
COLORSumi-2 (softer) on Washi-2 (recessed). The release is in the contrast drop, not in a different palette.
MOTIONDecelerates in. Holds longer than Hajime — 1.4–2s. The player is meant to sit with it. Then the coach window opens beneath.
PERMITSRest. The next-thing-to-do is now a choice, not a reaction. Instructions can be issued. Conversations can begin.
FREQUENCYMany times per match. Many times per session. Used as the deck's "section break" punctuation between Hajime-weight ideas.
The pair, not the calls. they live together
Hajime is never used without Matte being part of the system. The deck cover is Hajime; the closing slide is Matte. The press kit's first image is Hajime; the last sentence is Matte. They are parentheses around everything Hajime makes.
Never decoration. always punctuation
If a surface uses Hajime as a graphic flourish — a watermark, a pattern, a corner ornament — the system has been violated. The call only ever appears at the moment it would actually be called. Begin a thing. Suspend a thing. Nothing else.
08viii.

What the deck must not look like

The old deck's defaults, named explicitly so they cannot sneak back in.

DON'T · 01
Dark mode by default.

Hajime's surface is paper. Dark backgrounds belong to the in-game match overlay only — not slides, not the website, not the press kit. The deck is a document, not a console UI.

DOWashi #F3EDE0 surface, Sumi text.
DON'T#0E0E12 background with neon stat bars.
DON'T · 02
Stock-game iconography.

No swords, no dojo gates rendered in SVG, no katana silhouettes, no Japanese-flag motifs. The lineage is in the type and the stamp. Imagery is photographic, archival, or absent.

DOB&W archival photo, full-bleed, with caption.
DON'TSVG katana crossing a torii gate.
DON'T · 03
"Sim" stat decoration.

Hajime is a serious sim. The deck is not. Stat panels appear only where they are showing the actual game — never as decorative trim around a marketing claim.

DOOne real stat block, captioned as a screen.
DON'TThree radar charts of made-up numbers.
DON'T · 04
Loud color anywhere but the score.

Vermilion only on the things vermilion is for. Tatami green is the workhorse. If a slide needs three colors to communicate, the slide needs less content, not more color.

DOOne stamp of Shu per spread, max.
DON'TGradient backgrounds. Ever.
