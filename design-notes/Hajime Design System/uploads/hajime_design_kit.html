Visual identity · design kit

Hajime

A coaching simulation rooted in a specific basement on South Avenue West, Cranford, New Jersey.

Design exploration · April 17, 2026 · Three palette directions · Typography · Wordmark · Art direction · Tone

1The Source Dojo

Good identities come from a real place. Hajime's place is already picked out. Everything downstream traces back to it.

You walk down. The street noise drops. The air gets warmer. Old trophies catch the amber light on the far wall. A bell rings. Someone is already on the mat. This is where the game happens.

Cranford JKC — the Cranford Judo & Karate Center — sits below street level at 107 South Avenue West. Bsmt 1 in the mailing address. A literal basement sanctuary.

Founded by Yoshisada Yonezuka in 1962, two years after he emigrated from Japan. He coached the US Olympic Judo Team in 1988 and 1992. Six Olympians came out of that basement before he passed in 2014. His son Nicolas, himself a 1980 Olympian, runs it now. The current generation — Jack Yonezuka — competed in Paris 2024. Three generations of one family, sixty-four years of continuous instruction, one room underground.

This is Hajime's design-by-story anchor. The multigenerational dojo, the Wall of champions, the coach who retires and passes the club to a former student — those aren't imagined mechanics. They are what Cranford has been doing for sixty-four years. The game is already there. The identity should feel like walking down those steps.

The move. Editorial, museum-catalog, broadsheet-newspaper quality. The game styled like a book about judo, not a game of judo. Indigo dominant, rice paper for canvas, vermilion only for the hanko (name stamp) that finishes each match result. Brass for numeric data. Celadon as a quiet secondary for the mat.

Risk: can feel cold or remote. Distances the player from the sweat and basement warmth of Cranford. Best for menus and title screens; may fight against the match screen's intensity.

The move. This is less a palette than a method. The palette is whatever a real photograph of Cranford JKC contains. UI lives in a muted ink-and-stamp monochrome that doesn't fight the image. Every location in the game is a photograph, slightly desaturated, with paper-doll judoka drawn over it — the way Despelote renders Quito.

Strength: makes Hajime a personal artifact. The game is of your dojo. Requires you (or a friend with a camera) to actually photograph Cranford JKC — a weekend's work. Would be wildly original for a coaching sim.

3Typography

Two pairings. Both work with Palette 01. The first leans literary; the second leans broadcast.

Pairing A · literary · recommended for the sanctuary feel

Tanaka approaches. Right hand to the lapel.

Display and body in a warm old-style serif — Iowan Old Style, Palatino, or (if licensed) Hoefler Text. Reads like a book about judo, which is the whole tonal target. The match prose stream wants this. Numbers and grip-graph readouts sit in a mono for contrast.

0:14 · grip depth 0.8 · composure 7/10 · R.hand → DEEP COLLAR

Pairing B · broadcast · for tournament surfaces

Round 1 · –73kg · TANAKA vs. SATO

A thin uppercase sans — Helvetica Neue Light, Inter, or similar — for score strips and broadcast chrome. Used only where the game is pretending to be television. The sanctuary is serif. The match venue is sans. The contrast is the point.

5The Art Direction Question

Before picking a palette, a bigger question has to be answered. What kind of image is Hajime?

The Drawn Game

Ref: Wildermyth · Boxing Gym Story

Everything is hand-drawn. Tatami, dojo, judoka, coach chair, tournament hall — all 2D illustrated assets, all yours to shape. The paper-doll layering already sketched fits this.

Pro: total control. Unified style. Scales to many locations.

Con: more art to produce. Risk of feeling generic if the illustration isn't specific.

The Photograph Game

Ref: Despelote · Virginia · Kentucky Route Zero prologue

Backgrounds are real photographs — Cranford JKC's basement, a tournament venue, a weigh-in room. Judoka are drawn and layered over. Time of day, weather, dust in the air — all captured by lens, not illustrated.

Pro: instantly specific. The game is your dojo. Unimitable.

Con: requires access (Nick Yonezuka's permission, a photographer, a weekend). Binds the game to one real place.

The Isometric Game

Ref: Disco Elysium · Into the Breach

Everything rendered in 2D isometric tiles. Dojo, mat, coach chair, all placed on a grid. Sprites scaled accordingly. Cleaner spatial reasoning for the match view and training day view.

Pro: the scale-model aesthetic matches coaching's god's-eye nature. Easy to compose new rooms.

Con: can feel distanced. Loses the intimate basement warmth that the source dojo is built on.

The Hybrid

Ref: hand-drawn characters on photo backdrops

Photograph backdrops for locations (basement dojo, tournament hall, weigh-in). Hand-drawn paper-doll judoka layered on. Match-view specifically uses a warm-lit drawn tatami. UI floats above both in the Pairing A serif type.

This is probably the answer. Photo gives the specificity; drawn characters give the legibility the coaching sim needs. The drawn tatami in match view means you don't need perfectly-lit photos of mid-throw action.

7Tone of Voice

How Hajime talks — in menus, in match prose, on a tooltip, in the title screen tagline.

Is
Observational. Sportswriter. The game narrates what happened; it does not editorialize.
Is not
Hyped. No exclamation marks. No "EPIC" or "CRUSHED."
Is
Specific. "Right hand to the lapel." Not "grapples for grip."
Is not
Jargon-dense. Japanese terms are used where they are the real word (ippon, matte, kuzushi) and explained once, in-world.
Is
Reverent about the ritual. Hajime begins every match. The bow happens. The word matters.
Is not
Solemn to a fault. A match can end funny. A judoka can be young and goofy. The dojo has warmth.

8Recommendation

One pick, said plainly.

Direction: Palette 01 — Basement Sanctuary. Typography Pairing A — the literary serif. Art Direction: the Hybrid (photo backgrounds, drawn characters). Wordmark: Option A or C — the serif-based versions; the lowercase "hajime." has quiet personality and would age well.

The reason is simple: the source dojo is warm. Cranford JKC is not a cool, distant, museum-catalog place. It is amber light and trophy wood and three generations of a family teaching in a basement. If the identity tries to be Tokyo-editorial, it will be pretty and it will be wrong. The warmth is the whole thing. Lead with it.

The photograph question is real, and I think the answer is yes, eventually. If Nick Yonezuka ever says yes to letting you photograph the dojo — and it would be a conversation worth having — then the basement of Cranford JKC becomes the dojo of Hajime. The emotional payload of that is enormous. And if not, the warm drawn version of it still works.

Proposed next step: take this kit into Claude Design (if you have access) or back into this chat, and make two things — a title card and a match-screen mockup, both in this direction. Once those exist as images you'd actually print, the identity is locked.
