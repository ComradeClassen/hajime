Each judoka is generated with a country of origin, a club lineage, a body, a tokui-waza, a temperament, and a story. The simulation respects them. Some will be world-class. Some will quit after a month. You won't know which is which when they step through the door.

始HAJIME04 / 10 · Procedural

The simulation is the foundation

Three systems. One honest match.

The career layer floats on top of a match sim that respects judo: grips fight, kuzushi matters, throws have setups, scrambles go to the ground. Nothing is a dice roll with flavor text.

01

Grip fight

Every exchange starts at the hands. Who gets the lapel. Who gets the sleeve. Who breaks first. 70% of a real match is decided here — and so is 70% of the sim.

Models: hand dominance, grip fatigue, pistol vs. high-collar preference, griphand strength.

Tokui-waza · Kumi-kata
02

Kuzushi → throw

Balance is a state, not a probability. You break it with foot position, direction, and tempo. The throw is the conclusion, not the attempt.

Models: eight directions of off-balance, entry timing, uchi-komi familiarity per technique.

Nage-waza
03

Ne-waza

The ground is its own game. Pins, armbars, strangles. Position before submission. A bad scramble can cost you the match you were winning.

Models: position hierarchy, pin timers, escape windows, ne-waza specialist trait.

Ne-waza · Osaekomi
始HAJIME05 / 10 · Simulation
COACH'S CHAIR · RD 1 · 0:24

The player's role

You don't fight.
You coach.

The match plays itself. You watch, like Neil Adams in the booth, from the chair in the corner. When matte is called, you get the seconds between — two instructions, a look, a breath — and then hajime.

  • AYou never touch a d-pad during the match. You touch it between them.
  • BYour fighters hear you — within limits. Composure, fatigue, and their own stubbornness all filter the call.
  • COver a career, what you say to them becomes what they believe about themselves.
始HAJIME06 / 10 · Coach

What a key moment looks like

Between every exchange,
a Paradox-style event card.

Matte is called. The match pauses. The scene illustrates itself — your coach rising from the chair, the fighters walking back — and you get two instructions to spend before the next hajime.

Each instruction has a cost. Break his grip: +grip, −tempo. Stay patient: +composure. Over a career, these calls are the coaching relationship.

→ tone: Neil Adams on commentary. present tense. warm. technical.
