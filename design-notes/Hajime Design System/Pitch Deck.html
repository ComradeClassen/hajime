The referee's call

The word that starts every match. The bow is over, the grips have not yet engaged. One second of stillness before the work begins.

The game is everything that happens before the call is set, and everything that happens after.

It is also the call that opens the dojo. The match is one of many things that happens inside the world the call opens.

FOR THE STRANGER
What is judo?
A 19th-century Japanese martial art, now an Olympic sport. Two judoka in white gi grip each other's jackets and try to throw the other onto their back. A clean throw — ippon — ends the match instantly. There is etiquette: the bow, the silence, the referee's call.
What is a dojo?
The room where judo is taught. More than a gym. An institution with a name, a founding date, a lineage of teachers, a culture students sense the moment they step inside. People do not join a coach. They join a dojo.
What is a sensei?
The teacher who runs the dojo. In Hajime, this is who you play. Not a fighter — a coach. You sit beside the mat. You build the calendar. You decide who is ready, who is not, who needs a conversation this week and who needs to be left alone.
What kind of game is this?
A solo-developed simulation. Closer to Football Manager and Dwarf Fortress than to a fighting game. Most of the play is calendar, roster, conversations. The matches are the consequential events the dojo produces — beautiful when they come, but rare in the texture of a season.

If a slide later in the deck loses you, return here. The rest of the document assumes these four definitions and stops explaining them.

You run a dojo. You set the calendar. You spend the attention. The match is what the work eventually produces.

Procedurally generated students walk in. Some try a free week and disappear. Some plateau at yellow belt and stay for years. A few become champions.

Inside each session you choose where to spend your attention — watching one randori pair while four others run unwatched, or pulling a student aside for a conversation that begins to reveal who they are.

The dojo's culture emerges from your accumulated choices. The dojo's reputation shapes who walks through the door next. Every so often, a tournament arrives.

The inversion

The match engine that Ring 1 has been building is still real and still load-bearing — but it lives inside the dojo loop now, not above it.

Five principles.

THE LOAD-BEARING IDEASEach principle is a constraint
and a permission, in the same line.
i/v
Attention is scarce, and that is the game.

Five pairs of randori, one sensei. You can watch one. The other four run unwatched and you will never know what happened. What you choose to watch is what you choose to know.

SCARCITY · ATTENTION
ii/v
The dojo learns from cultural feedback, not just performance.

Reputation is built by what visitors and rivals say about your room. Atmosphere, pricing, the way you handle a loss — this is what changes who walks in next.

CULTURE · LOOP
iii/v
Continuous simulation, render on demand.

The world keeps running whether you are watching or not. Students train, rest, doubt, improve. You render the slice you choose — the rest is computed and waiting when you turn your head.

ENGINE · TIME
iv/v
Outcomes emerge from composition, not scripting.

No quest tree, no hand-authored arc. A judoka's career — and the dojo's — is what falls out of the procedural inputs colliding over years.

EMERGENCE · NO SCRIPTING
v/v
Either choice is okay.

No optimal path. No best build. The mid-tier dojo that produces one happy lifelong yellow belt is not lesser than the dojo that produces an Olympian. Both endings are the game.

The six cultural inputs.

WHAT VISITORS FEELThe dojo's reputation isn't a stat —
it is the sum of these six choices.
A judoka chooses your dojo for reasons that are mostly not about your win-loss record. They walk in, they breathe the room, they ask their friend about the sensei. This is what they're reading.
稽
INPUT · 01
Session content
What you actually teach. The ratio of drilling to randori, the techniques you favor, whether you correct in the moment or after.
円
INPUT · 02
Pricing & access
Free first week, family rates, the kid whose parents can't pay this month. Who can afford to be in the room.
系
INPUT · 03
Lineage & style
Whose judo are you teaching — your father's, your own departure from it, a school you imported. The unbroken chain students sense without being told.
気
INPUT · 04
Atmosphere on the mat
Quiet and reverent, loud and joking, brutally serious. The thing you cannot fake. Set by how you behave when something goes wrong.
昇
INPUT · 05
Promotion philosophy
When does a student get the next belt — at performance, at time, at attitude? What does your dojo say a black belt is worth?
試
INPUT · 06
Competition readiness
The pattern of who you send to which tournaments, and what you say to them on the way home — win or lose.

Twenty-year arc. Years on the axis, not real-time dates. Most of the play happens between the marked stops — sessions, conversations, weeks that look like nothing.

WHAT THESE AREAnchoring Scenes

The three red dots are the moments the master doc commits to. The basement opening, the first team tournament, the Olympic walk-on. Everything else is procedural and emergent.

None of these are required to happen. A dojo that produces one happy lifelong yellow belt is also the game. The Olympic moment is a possibility, not a milestone.

The inner-life mechanic.

SYSTEM 01 · STUDENTWhat a procedurally-generated
judoka wants, fears, and works
toward across fifteen seasons

Every student in the dojo runs the same loop. Needs in the moment, goals across the years, and a quiet machine for changing what they want. The mechanic underneath every conversation, every randori, every promotion you have to think about.

SYSTEM 01

Immediate needs

The texture of being alive at this hour.

This week's stamina. Last session's bruise. The conversation with their parent on Sunday night. The thing they ate before practice and the song stuck in their head on the way over.

SYSTEM 02

Long-term goals

What they're working toward across the years.

Black belt by 30. Beat their older sister at randori. Not quit before their father comes to watch a tournament. The private list each judoka carries that the dojo never sees.

SYSTEM 03

Goal evolution

How a person changes shape over a decade.

What happens when they get the black belt and don't feel different. The pivot from champion to teacher. The quiet exit at 27. The unexpected return at 38.

SYSTEM 04

Lineage as data

Whose judo lives in their body.

Your sensei's sensei's notes, the technique you taught them that came from a tournament loss in 2008, the throw they keep trying because their first coach loved it. Memory carried in muscle.

Why it matters. Every other system in the dojo — randori, conversations, promotions, departures — reads from this. Without it, the room is a graphics layer with no people in it.
